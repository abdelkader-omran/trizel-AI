Extended Description

AUTO DZ ACT is a scientific algorithm designed for epistemic validation. Its purpose is not to construct theories, optimize models, or interpret physical causes, but to determine whether a given scientific claim is experimentally decidable under explicitly stated conditions.

The algorithm operates by enforcing a strict separation between:

  • Theoretical output (T) — what a claim or model asserts should be observed
  • Empirical or observational data (E) — what is actually measured or recorded
  • Decision thresholds — explicitly declared tolerances and comparison criteria

Given these inputs, AUTO DZ ACT deterministically assigns the claim to one of four formal epistemic states:

  • 0/0 — Confirmed: Agreement between theory and data within declared thresholds
  • D0/DZ — Indeterminate: Data exist but lack sufficient discriminative power
  • DZ — Contradicted: Significant discrepancy under declared criteria
  • ∞/∞ — Not Testable: Absence of comparable, well-defined, or accessible data

AUTO DZ ACT is deterministic, version-locked, and audit-oriented. It does not assign probabilities, confidence scores, or rankings, and it does not select between competing theoretical frameworks. Its role is limited to answering a single question:

Is this claim scientifically testable, undecidable, contradicted, or currently untestable given the available data and declared criteria?

The algorithm treats the absence of measurement as a first-class scientific outcome. This prevents speculative inference, retrospective reinterpretation, and premature theoretical consolidation in data-limited or frontier research contexts.

AUTO DZ ACT is intentionally model-agnostic and applicable across domains, including but not limited to fundamental physics, astronomy, experimental sciences, and any field where theory–experiment alignment is nontrivial.

AUTO DZ ACT is a deterministic, verification-first epistemic validation algorithm. It evaluates scientific claims by comparing theoretical outputs (T) to empirical or observational data (E) under explicitly declared thresholds, then assigns one of four epistemic states: 0/0 (Confirmed), D0/DZ (Indeterminate), DZ (Contradicted), ∞/∞ (Not Testable).
